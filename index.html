Interactive Effects of Genetic Liability and Combat Exposure on Risk of Alcohol Use Disorder Among US Service Members

Drug and Alcohol Dependence

Integrating information on genetic predisposition for alcohol use disorder (i.e., polygenic risk scores) with information on service members history and combat exposure, we found that the effect of combat exposure on problematic alcohol consumption varies in part due to the influence of genetic vulnerability. This work exemplifies the importance of understanding “Environment X Genetic” effects and provides a framework to help enhance preventative efforts by enabling more precise targeting of aid to soldiers.

A person-centered approach to behaving badly at work: An examination of workplace deviance patterns

Journal of Applied Psychology

This manuscript challenges traditional views of workplace deviance by using a person-centered approach to uncover distinct patterns of deviant behavior at work. Across two large studies, the authors identify five unique classes of employees based on how they engage in harmful behaviors, and show that these classes are associated with different personality traits, experiences of unfairness, and workplace outcomes. The findings offer a more nuanced understanding of why and how people behave badly at work, moving beyond simple categories like “interpersonal” or “organizational” deviance.

Caring for Their Own: How Firm Actions to Protect Essential Workers and CEO Benevolence Influenced Stakeholder Sentiment During the COVID-19 Pandemic

Journal of Applied Psychology

At the onset of the COVID-19 pandemic, firms varied considerably in the degree to which they took action to protect their employees’ safety and financial well-being, and stakeholders took notice. In this study, we examined how firm leadership – in the form of action and perceptions of benevolence – helped shape stakeholder sentiment. Our longitudinal analysis of positive stakeholder sentiment expressed on social media demonstrated the importance of immediate firm action. Specifically, firm compensation actions were associated with a growth in positive sentiment, particularly when made by CEOs with high benevolence, whereas firm safety actions led to growth in positive sentiment but only when made by CEOs with low benevolence.

Reflecting on Death Amidst COVID-19 and Individual Creativity

Journal of Applied Psychology

Death awareness was a very real experience during the initial peak of the COVID-19 pandemic, and it brought unexpected consequences. This paper investigates the impact of death awareness on creativity in the workplace. Using weekly panel data, we found that death reflection (thinking deeply about mortality) was positively linked to creativity, while death anxiety (fear/worry over one's mortality) had no statistically significant effect. The study highlights how even during crisis periods, certain types of reflection can lead to positive psychological and behavioral outcomes.

Trust Momentum: A new understanding of dynamic trust

This manuscript explores dynamic trust through the subjective perspective of time and argues that the rate and direction of trust development — called trust momentum — influences future interpersonal dynamics at work. Rather than just examining static levels of trust, we argue that trust momentum signals meaningful interpersonal cues. Experiencing growing trust or declining trust prompts feelings of hope or fear, respectively, that feed into consideration of trust in the moment. This momentum-based view challenges traditional models by emphasizing that the nature in which trust evolves may be as psychologically and behaviorally impactful as how much trust is ultimately achieved.

Finalizing for: Academy of Management Journal

The importance of first impressions and events that change them: How does trust anchor and change over time

This multi-study investigation explores how trust develops over time and highlights the powerful role of positive critical trust events—pivotal moments that can rapidly and durably accelerate trust growth. By shifting focus from the well-studied dynamics of trust violations, the research demonstrates that early, high-stakes positive events not only boost trust immediately but also enhance its long-term trajectory. The findings offer practical implications for accelerating trust in dynamic work environments, such as during onboarding, leadership transitions, or the formation of temporary teams.

About Jason Kautz

I am an Assistant Professor of Organizational Behavior and Human Resource Management at the Jindal School of Management at the University of Texas at Dallas. My research is at the intersection of interpersonal dynamics, trust, and counterproductive work behaviors. I apply innovative methodologies to uncover the hidden patterns shaping how people work together or fall apart.

I grew up in Buffalo, New York. When I was 18, I joined the Navy and traveled the world on the aircraft carrier the USS Carl Vinson (CVN 70). Since then, I have traveled and made home in several areas.

In my free time, I enjoy cooking, gardening, and reading sci-fi novels. I am also newly discovering my passion for hiking and camping. When traveling, I prioritize trying out local coffee and visiting natural history museums when possible (dinosaurs rock!).
